01

The problem

The economy runs on activity that financial systems cannot see.

Across the world, the majority of economic activity happens outside formal financial infrastructure. Entrepreneurs earn income, pay suppliers, serve customers, and build businesses every day. What they rarely build is a paper trail that financial institutions recognize.

In the United States, the FDIC reported 5.6 million unbanked households and 19.0 million underbanked households in 2023 — cash-based entrepreneurs in Chicago, Detroit, Houston, and across the country generating real income with no formal record. The IFC estimates a $5.7 trillion finance gap for small and medium enterprises globally, rising to $8 trillion when informal enterprises are included.

The scale is global. In sub-Saharan Africa, more than 80% of employment is informal. In Latin America and the Caribbean, roughly half of working people operate outside the formal economy. Across Europe, platform and gig workers increasingly fall outside traditional credit assessment frameworks.

The consequence is the same everywhere: lenders cannot evaluate what they cannot see. Creditworthy businesses are declined. Programs designed to serve underserved communities lack the documentation to reach them. The gap is not a failure of ambition. It is a failure of infrastructure.

02

The solution

Record, review, confirm, export.

01

Record

Log a sale, payment, or expense by voice or text. No forms, accounting labels, or new bookkeeping habits are required.

02

Review

See a structured summary of the entry before committing, so the user controls what becomes part of the record.

03

Confirm

A single confirmation makes the record permanent. Once confirmed, it cannot be changed or backdated.

04

Export

Generate a verified financial history report for lenders, programs, auditors, or regulators.

Konfirmata works offline, is designed for voice-first and low-literacy use, and structures records for independent verification. The app is live now.

04

Security and trust

Trust by architecture, not policy.

Permanent Records

Once confirmed, an entry cannot be altered, deleted, or backdated. This is structural, not a policy setting.

Independent Verification

Every export contains data that allows independent review of record integrity without requiring access to the original phone or Konfirmata servers.

User-Controlled Data

Users own their records. Exports belong to the user, and no institution receives them without user authorization.

Offline-First Design

Records can be created and confirmed without internet connectivity, then prepared for review when connectivity is available.

The core architecture is permanent by design, not by policy.

Inventor Story

Built from two sides of the same problem.

Muhammad Al-Amin Bamalli observed the problem from inside formal financial infrastructure. As a Principal Manager in the Banking Supervision Department of the Central Bank of Nigeria, conducting risk-based examinations of the country's largest banks, he understood precisely how institutions evaluate trust, what documentation they require, and why capable businesses were turned away not for lack of performance, but for lack of records. The problem was not creditworthiness. It was invisibility.

He brought that regulatory depth into U.S. financial markets, completing a Master of Science in Financial Analysis at Temple University's Fox School of Business, a CFA Institute-affiliated program, and working as a senior financial analyst at a major U.S. financial institution. The architecture of Konfirmata reflects what he learned across both environments: what it takes to build records that institutions will actually trust.

Ahmed Yaradua contributed the co-inventor's perspective: grounded understanding of how informal markets operate, what operators in those markets need from a verification system, and what trust looks like from the user's side of the gap. His contribution shaped the practical design of the system.

Konfirmata is not a bookkeeping app. It is financial identity infrastructure, designed to make the invisible visible, in a form that lenders, regulators, and auditors can rely on.
