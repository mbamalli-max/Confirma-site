The problem

The economy runs on activity that financial systems cannot see.

Across the world, the majority of economic activity happens outside formal financial infrastructure. Market traders in Lagos, platform workers in London, cash-based entrepreneurs in Chicago, smallholder farmers in Nairobi — they earn income, pay suppliers, serve customers, and build businesses every day. What they rarely build is a paper trail that financial institutions recognize.

The International Finance Corporation estimates a $5.7 trillion finance gap for small and medium enterprises globally — rising to $8 trillion when informal enterprises are included. In sub-Saharan Africa, more than 80% of employment is informal. Across Latin America and the Caribbean, the figure exceeds 50%. In Europe, the growth of platform and gig work is outpacing credit assessment frameworks designed for traditional employment.

In the United States — where Konfirmata is built and where its national impact is anchored — the FDIC reported 5.6 million unbanked households and 19 million underbanked in 2023. The CFPB estimates 7 million U.S. adults hold no credit record at all, with another 9.8% holding records that produce no usable score. The Federal Reserve Small Business Credit Survey found that low-risk Latino-owned firms received full financing at roughly half the rate of comparable white-owned firms — not because of performance, but because of documentation gaps.

The consequence is the same everywhere: lenders cannot evaluate what they cannot see. Creditworthy businesses are declined. Programs designed to reach underserved communities lack the records to serve them. The gap is not a failure of ambition — it is a failure of infrastructure.

02

The solution

Record, review, confirm, export.

01

Record

Log a sale, payment, or expense by voice or text. No forms, accounting labels, or new bookkeeping habits are required.

02

Review

See a structured summary of the entry before committing, so the user controls what becomes part of the record.

03

Confirm

A single confirmation makes the record permanent. Once confirmed, it cannot be changed or backdated.

04

Export

Generate a verified financial history report for lenders, programs, auditors, or regulators.

Konfirmata works offline, is designed for voice-first and low-literacy use, and structures records for independent verification. The app is live now.

For lenders and institutions

Supplemental underwriting input for borrowers with real activity and thin documentation.

Konfirmata does not replace existing underwriting. It fills a data gap for CDFIs, credit unions, community banks, micro-lenders, SBA lenders, and nonprofit lending programs evaluating borrowers who have activity but lack formal documentation.

For credit committees, the value is practical: structured records, continuity indicators, exportable summaries, and an audit-ready review path for borrower-provided history.

04

Security and trust

Trust by architecture, not policy.

Permanent Records

Once confirmed, an entry cannot be altered, deleted, or backdated. This is structural, not a policy setting.

Independent Verification

Every export contains data that allows independent review of record integrity without requiring access to the original phone or Konfirmata servers.

User-Controlled Data

Users own their records. Exports belong to the user, and no institution receives them without user authorization.

Offline-First Design

Records can be created and confirmed without internet connectivity, then prepared for review when connectivity is available.

The core architecture is permanent by design, not by policy.

Founder & Co-inventor

Built from two sides of the same problem.

Muhammad Al-Amin Bamalli observed the problem from inside formal financial infrastructure. As a Principal Manager in the Banking Supervision Department of the Central Bank of Nigeria — conducting risk-based examinations of the country's largest financial institutions — he understood precisely how banks evaluate trust, what documentation they require, and why capable businesses were turned away not for lack of performance, but for lack of records. The problem was not creditworthiness. It was invisibility.

He brought that regulatory depth into U.S. financial markets, completing a Master of Science in Financial Analysis at Temple University's Fox School of Business — a CFA Institute-affiliated program — and working as a senior financial analyst at a major U.S. financial institution. The architecture of Konfirmata reflects what he learned across both environments: what institutional trust actually requires, and what it would take to build records that meet that standard from the ground up.

Ahmed Yaradua contributed the co-inventor's perspective: grounded understanding of how informal markets operate, what operators in those markets need from a verification system, and what trust looks like from the user's side of the gap. His contribution shaped the practical design of the system — ensuring that Konfirmata works for the people it is built to serve, not only for the institutions that will eventually rely on it.

Together they hold USPTO Patent Application No. 63/987,858. Konfirmata is the result — not a bookkeeping app, but financial identity infrastructure designed to make the invisible visible in a form that lenders, regulators, and auditors can rely on.
